# Run Playbook

## Purpose

This playbook describes how to execute a repeatable Signal Check preflight run.

A run is successful when it produces a clearer real-world next test, not when it produces a positive answer.

## Inputs

Before starting, collect:

- product idea or product brief
- intended audience or audience hypotheses
- problem or job-to-be-done hypothesis
- constraints
- known risks
- alternative solutions
- what the team is tempted to believe
- what the team is afraid to learn

## Outputs

A complete run produces:

- run card
- sampling plan
- stimuli
- negative controls
- prompt robustness notes
- synthesis
- quality review
- decision log
- Smallest Real Anchor

## Step 1 — Write the product brief

The product brief should describe who might care, what problem or job is involved, what the product proposes, what is uncertain, and what must not be claimed.

Avoid polished pitch language. The brief should be specific enough to be tested, but not so persuasive that every downstream reaction is shaped by marketing copy.

Failure signs:

- The brief sounds like a landing page.
- The target audience is everyone.
- The problem is described only from the builder's point of view.
- The brief assumes willingness to pay.

Recovery: rewrite the brief as a neutral working hypothesis.

## Step 2 — Choose segments

Select a small panel of perspectives:

- likely-fit segment
- adjacent segment
- skeptical segment
- poor-fit segment
- non-market control where appropriate

Segment descriptions should be grounded in contexts, constraints, and jobs. Do not invent detailed fake people.

Failure signs:

- All segments are fans.
- Segments differ only by demographics.
- No segment is allowed to reject the idea.

Recovery: add a skeptical segment and a poor-fit segment before continuing.

## Step 3 — Define stimuli

Stimuli are alternative ways to explain the idea.

Recommended set:

- category-led stimulus
- problem-led stimulus
- action-led stimulus
- skeptical or negative-control stimulus

Failure signs:

- Every stimulus is a better-written version of the same pitch.
- The negative control is obviously absurd.
- Stimuli imply proof, popularity, or demand.

Recovery: rewrite stimuli to vary the claim, angle, or promise being tested.

## Step 4 — Define negative controls

Negative controls help detect whether the run is too agreeable.

Use controls that should not perform well if the method is working:

- overclaiming stimulus
- vague hype stimulus
- wrong audience stimulus
- unsupported partnership claim
- too-broad value proposition

Failure signs:

- Negative controls are rated as strong without caveats.
- The synthesis ignores negative-control performance.
- The run treats all positive reactions equally.

Recovery: pause the run and run a bias audit.

## Step 5 — Run neutral reactions

Use prompts that allow refusal, confusion, skepticism, indifference, and rejection.

Capture reactions as directional synthetic responses, not user quotes.

Do not write them as testimonials. Do not attribute them to real users.

## Step 6 — Audit bias

Inspect whether the run is producing agreeable output by default.

Check for response collapse, shallow praise, overconfident synthesis, missing objections, negative controls passing too easily, and prompt wording that pushes toward approval.

## Step 7 — Synthesize without cherry-picking

The synthesis must include:

- what was understood
- what remained unclear
- what seemed useful
- what was rejected
- what objections repeated
- what differed by segment
- what the negative controls revealed
- what cannot be concluded

The synthesis should not contain a product verdict.

## Step 8 — Apply kill criteria

Kill criteria should be defined before interpreting the run.

Examples:

- target segment cannot explain the problem back in plain language
- skeptical segment raises a blocking trust issue
- poor-fit segment likes it more than likely-fit segment
- strongest stimulus depends on an unsupported claim
- Smallest Real Anchor cannot be made concrete

## Step 9 — Decide the run outcome

Use one of these outcomes:

- continue to real test
- revise framing
- narrow segment
- clarify problem
- pause idea
- kill current version

Avoid binary good idea or bad idea decisions.

## Step 10 — Define the Smallest Real Anchor

The run must end with a real-world test.

A Smallest Real Anchor includes who will be exposed to the idea, what they will see or do, what behavior or response will be observed, what threshold matters, what would make the team stop or revise, and how the result will be documented.

A run is complete only when uncertainty is visible, negative controls are interpreted, no demand proof is claimed, no real-user language is invented, kill criteria are visible, and the Smallest Real Anchor is specific enough to execute.
