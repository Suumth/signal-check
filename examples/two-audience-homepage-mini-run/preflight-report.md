# Preflight Report: Two-Audience Homepage
## Decision
Reframe.
## Summary
The strongest homepage architecture is likely:
1. product-lab frame,
2. concrete proof product early,
3. method behind the proof,
4. broader lab or future work after the proof is understood.
This is a directional preflight interpretation, not validation.
## What this run can say
The comparison suggests that a homepage serving two audiences should not start too abstractly and should not collapse into a single-product page.
A concrete proof product can make the broader lab more credible when shown early.
## What this run cannot say
This run cannot prove:
- that real visitors will understand the page
- that buyers will convert
- that professional visitors will find the builder credible
- that the story will work in real traffic
- that the product lab has market demand
## What this run still does not know
This run still does not know:
- whether real buyers understand the proof product quickly
- whether professional visitors understand the broader product-lab frame
- whether the method section increases trust or creates friction
- whether the homepage needs different paths for different visitors
- whether the strongest message works outside the prompt environment
## What to keep
- Concrete proof product early.
- Restrained, confident language.
- Clear method boundaries.
- Product quality as credibility signal.
## What to change
- Avoid abstract brand-first language unless immediately grounded by proof.
- Avoid method-first language before the practical example is clear.
- Avoid personal portfolio framing.
## What to stop
- Do not lead with vague AI-studio positioning.
- Do not imply validation or user proof.
- Do not let the proof product erase the broader lab.
## Segment differences
| Segment | Likely reaction pattern | Risk |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Practical product buyer | Needs concrete product clarity early. | May ignore abstract lab language. |
| Professional credibility visitor | Looks for judgment, method, and execution quality. | May find proof-product-only framing too narrow. |
| Skeptical visitor | Rejects vague premium or AI language. | Needs bounded claims and proof. |
| Poor-fit agency prospect | Wants services or client work. | May misunderstand the lab as an agency. |
| Non-market control | Should not show strong meaningful fit. | Strong support here would reduce confidence. |
## Negative-control readout
The AI-studio, portfolio, proof-only, and method-only controls are useful because they expose four different failure modes:
- vague abstraction
- self-promotional portfolio energy
- narrow product-only framing
- method without proof
## Claims not allowed from this run
- No validated purchase intent.
- No representative findings.
- No real user voices.
- No demand prediction.
- No synthetic testimonials.
- No launch-readiness claim.
- No claim that real visitors prefer this structure.
## Smallest Real Anchor
Run a small real-reader homepage comprehension test with two groups:
1. practical product buyers
2. professional credibility visitors
Use two or three homepage wireframe variants and ask what each page is, what it offers, what they trust, what is unclear, and what they would click next.
