# Signal Check Rules

Signal Check is a file-based method kit for product preflight work. It helps builders examine an idea before building by making assumptions, segments, objections, risks, negative controls, and the next real test explicit.

## What Signal Check is

- A structured preflight method for product ideas.
- A way to reduce self-conviction before building.
- A way to create better questions, sharper segments, and safer next tests.
- A file-based kit that can be used manually.
- A method that treats synthetic reactions as directional prompts, not evidence of a market.

## What Signal Check is not

Signal Check does not:

- validate demand
- prove purchase intent
- replace real user research
- produce representative findings
- simulate real users
- must not generate synthetic testimonials
- provide market-size evidence
- guarantee product success
- turn synthetic reactions into validated insight

## Claim boundaries

Allowed language:

- directional signal
- hypothesis
- synthetic reaction
- possible objection
- risk to test
- next real test
- assumption to verify

Not allowed:

- validated demand
- representative result
- proven purchase intent
- Do not write: users said
- market confirmed
- statistically significant
- accurate prediction
- customer testimonial

## Data minimization

Use public-safe, anonymized, low-detail inputs. Do not upload private customer data, confidential product plans, internal financials, personal data, or proprietary research unless the run is explicitly private and the user accepts the risk.

## Segment-first rule

Work with segments and contexts, not fake personas. A valid run must include:

- a fitting segment
- an adjacent segment
- a skeptical segment
- a poor-fit segment
- a non-market control

## Negative controls

Every run needs negative controls. A negative control is a deliberately unsuitable or misleading stimulus, segment, or claim used to detect over-positive or collapsed reactions.

If a negative control performs too well, slow down or stop. Do not explain it away.

## Kill criteria

Every run needs kill criteria before analysis. Kill criteria state what would make the idea, segment, message, or proposed next test unsafe to continue without revision.

## Smallest Real Anchor

Every run ends with a Smallest Real Anchor: the smallest real-world test that can create non-synthetic evidence.

It must define:

- who will be approached
- what they will see or do
- where the test happens
- what observable signal matters
- what threshold suggests continue, revise, or stop
- what kill criterion applies
- when the decision will be made

## Synthesis rules

A synthesis must:

- separate synthetic reactions from real evidence
- preserve objections
- include counter-evidence
- identify uncertainty
- avoid demand language
- avoid false precision
- end in a real test recommendation

## Example-safety rules

Public examples must be small, generic, and data-minimal. They must not include private data, proprietary product details, customer quotes, internal screenshots, or long raw response logs.

## Stop conditions

Stop or slow the run if:

- the brief is too vague to segment
- negative controls react too positively
- responses collapse into generic agreement
- the user asks for validation language
- the synthesis hides objections
- the next test is not real
- outputs imply representative findings
- private or sensitive data appears
